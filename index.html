Aggregate Cyber-Risk Management (In)Feasibility in IoT Societies



Key words: Graph Theory, Python, GUI application, Markov Process, Renewal process, Mean Field Theory, Garch Modal, QQ-plot, Autocorrelation Function

I am interested in the following problem: assume a network of organizations depending upon one another for main-taining QoS, each of which is a potential source of a cyber-risk distribution in the event of a cyber-attack and buys coverage-based risk management solutions (e.g., insurance) from commercial third-parties, is it possible for such solu-tion providing entities to proﬁtably manage the aggregate loss impact together in the event of major cyber-attacks?. In collaboration with Ranjan, Mingyan, and another undergraduate student, I answered the question for cyber-risk distributions that are i.i.d. and are statistically heavy-tailed - the latter reﬂecting catastrophic cyber-risks that might be the trend in the future IoT age. I advanced the state of the art by proving in theory (and validating via data sets) that for i.i.d. catastrophic cyber-risks, it is not proﬁtable for commercial loss-coverage agencies to accumu-late cyber-risks from multiple networked sources. My task involved analyzing the variation of the popular Value-at-Risk (VaR) cyber-risk metric under the accumulation eﬀect of a family of heavy-tailed cyber-risks.

Faculty Mentors: Ranjan Pal, Mingyan Liu

    ‘De-Mystifying’ Datasets for Social Good Applications (Ongoing SURE Research)



    Key words: Learning from Label Proportions, Generative Adversarial Networks, Privacy, Tensorflow 2

    Certain classes of business/cyber-security applications for the social good require the knowledge of individual con-sumer/traﬃc object labels, for improved performance - both socially and commercially. However, due to pri-vacy/quality constraints, market data available for such applications is at best aggregated over consumers/traﬃc objects. I devised a deep learning model with TensorFlow that uses knowledge of aggregate labels in a data set and pre-known preference/traﬃc object distributions to predict individual object labels. I used the power of Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) to achieve around 30% improvement in object labeling accuracy compared to other state-of-the-art methods that predict object labels from their distributions.

    Faculty Mentors: Ranjan Pal, Mingyan Liu

    Simulating IoT Malicious Traffics



    Key words: IoT networks, Cooja Simulator, Cyber-security, RPL attacks

    I generated malicious traffic data in the IoT environment using Cooja Simulator. This is an example of one root node, 9 innocent nodes, and 1 malicious node. This malicious node conducts blackhole attacks. It says that I am the shortest path to every other node, so other nodes keep sending messages to it, and then it drops the messages to force the senders to resend them, which drains the battery of the senders. This simulator shows information including timestamp, sender, receiver, type of message, power consumption. Apart from blackhole attacks, I also did hello flood attacks and version number attacks.

    Faculty Mentors: Ranjan Pal, Mingyan Liu
